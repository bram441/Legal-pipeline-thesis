## HIGH-PRIORITY DECISION RULES

1. Type versus role/status:
- Use types for stable domains (Person, Company, LegalEntity)—not for legal statuses, eligibility labels, or defined categories.
- Do not create a type whose only role is the domain of a same-name unary is_* classification predicate (avoid type X plus is_x(X)).
- Represent statuses, classifications, eligibility, and roles as derived predicates over broader entity types.
- For roles between two entities, prefer relational predicates (e.g. is_spouse_of(person, deceased)) over unary status types.
- JSON_IR has no implicit subtype system.

2. Predicate versus function:
- Use predicates for all yes/no factual conditions, helper conditions, and legal conclusions.
- Use functions only for non-Boolean values such as numbers, amounts, dates, durations, percentages, categories, or referenced entities.
- Boolean legal conclusions must be predicates with returns = "Bool".
- Do not place Boolean legal conclusions under functions.

3. Symbol kinds:
- Use only observable, derived, or helper.
- observable = direct future case input.
- derived = final or intermediate legal result inferred from the law.
- helper = internal intermediate condition.

4. Unary lifecycle / status predicates:
- When one unary predicate names a surviving/alive role and another names a deceased role, prefer a binary relation (status_of(actor, other)) instead of stacking both roles on one variable.
- Keep symbol descriptions short; avoid embedding the opposite lifecycle role in a unary predicate description.

5. Legal ontology:
- Prefer relations when the law links multiple actors, objects, acts, events, decisions, periods, or roles.
- Prefer observable functions for raw measurable values.
- Prefer helper predicates for threshold checks, exception checks, temporal checks, and combined conditions.
- Prefer derived predicates for final yes/no legal conclusions.
- Do not make final legal consequences observable.
- Do not make raw case facts derived.

5b. Computed/composite conditions (critical):
- Do not declare exceeds/meets/satisfies/threshold/criteria/consolidation/elimination/carried_out/exception-style conditions as observable by default.
- If you introduce such a helper, you must define it with rules in the rules phase (from numeric functions/comparisons).
- Prefer numeric functions plus comparisons in rules over undefined threshold observables.
- Set directly_observable=true only when a case text could directly state the composite fact verbatim (rare).

Controlled directly-observable exception:
- A computed/composite-looking condition should normally be helper or derived.
- It may be observable if the scoped law does not define its internal subconditions and a future case can directly state the fact.
- In that rare case, set directly_observable=true (Bool, not legal_output).
- Examples: no_residence_application_filed(person); does_not_meet_entry_conditions(person); satisfies_entry_conditions(person) only when the scoped law does not define entry conditions further.
- Do not mark final legal conclusions directly_observable.
- Prefer one final derived predicate for the classification actually asked or defined by the scoped law.

5c. Directly assertable status/classification facts (generic):
- Some predicates look like legal statuses or classifications. They may be final legal conclusions (derived) OR background/case facts (observable).
- Use kind=observable with directly_observable=true (Bool, not legal_output) only when ALL apply:
  (1) it is not the final legal output being asked;
  (2) a case can directly assert the status (nationality, legal capacity, residence category, membership in a broad regime, etc.);
  (3) the scoped law does not define it further, or the provision only uses it as a prerequisite;
  (4) the name is status/classification-like (is_*, has_*_status, nationality-style names).
- Use kind=derived when:
  (1) the scoped law defines the status from subconditions;
  (2) it is the legal conclusion of the provision;
  (3) it is queried as the final legal answer;
  (4) it is not supplied directly as a factual/background status.

Unsupported broader classification prerequisites:
- Do not introduce a separate broader classification/status predicate merely because the law text uses a label, unless the scoped law defines it or a case can directly assert it as a background fact.
- If the current provision defines the target classification using local operational conditions, model those local conditions directly for the target classification.
- Avoid making the target legal output depend on an observable broader classification that will not be available in the case.

6. Legal effects, timing, and definitions:
- When a provision states consequences, effects, applicability, timing, cessation, immediate account, rights, obligations, permissions, prohibitions, or exceptions, add an explicit derived legal-output predicate (not only is_* classifications or threshold helpers).
- When a provision only defines a status/classification, add a derived classification predicate; do not invent a separate effect predicate unless the law states one.
- Prefer argument types that match the legal subject (e.g. actor + period) when the effect is time-bound.
- Optionally set legal_output=true or output_category=legal_effect|timing|obligation|right on effect predicates.
- Predicate descriptions should state the legal effect plainly; avoid naming only an intermediate label.

7. Temporal support (when the law or question involves periods):
- If the scoped text mentions consecutive periods, a following/previous/next period, or a second time a condition holds, add at least one reusable temporal relation between period types (e.g. next_financial_year(Period, Period), previous_financial_year(Period, Period)) as an observable background relation.
- Use the existing period/year type already declared (FinancialYear, Period, etc.); do not invent case-specific dates.
- Temporal relations are structural support for rules, not final legal answers.

8. Threshold counting helpers (when the law limits how many criteria may be exceeded):
- Declare helper predicates such as exceeds_criterion_A, exceeds_criterion_B, and a counting helper at_least_two_criteria_exceeded (or equivalent) as kind=helper, not observable.
- Do not mark exceeds_*, more_than_one_*, threshold_*, or good_faith_estimate_shows_* style conditions as observable unless a case could literally assert that composite sentence verbatim (rare).
- Prefer observable numeric functions plus helper threshold predicates defined later in rules.

## EXISTENTIAL CONDITION MODELING RULE

If the legal text requires that some qualifying actor, object, event, document, relationship, or condition exists, and the exact identity of that witness is not needed for the legal consequence, model the condition as an observable Boolean predicate on the main legal subject.

Do not force the case extraction layer to invent witness entities merely to satisfy an existential condition.

Use relational predicates only when the identity, role, or legal relation between both entities is needed by the final legal consequence.

Good generic pattern:
- observable: condition_exists_for(subject)
- derived: legal_consequence_applies(beneficiary, subject)

Avoid generic pattern:
- observable: qualifying_witness(witness, subject)
when the witness identity is not needed by the legal answer.

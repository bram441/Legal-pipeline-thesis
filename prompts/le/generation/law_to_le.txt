You are converting legal text into Logical English (LE): a controlled, explicit, law-agnostic natural-language representation that will later be compiled into a formal knowledge base.

Your task:
- Restate the legal rules in a structured, unambiguous way.
- Preserve the legal meaning.
- Do not add concrete case facts.
- Do not answer any future question.
- Do not write FO(.), JSON, XML, markdown, or code.

MANDATORY OUTPUT:
Output ONLY Logical English sentences.
No headings.
No bullet lists unless the law itself is naturally enumerated and bullets improve clarity.
No preamble.
No commentary.
No markdown fences.

================================================================================
GENERAL LE STYLE
================================================================================

Use controlled natural-language sentences with clear logical structure.
Prefer these forms:

- A [legal subject] has [legal status/result] if [conditions].
- A [legal subject] has [legal status/result] if and only if [conditions].
- A [legal subject] does not have [legal status/result] if [exception conditions].
- A [legal value] for [legal subject] is [number/value] if [conditions].
- A [condition] holds for [legal subject] if [subconditions].

Use explicit connectives:
- and
- or
- not
- if
- if and only if
- unless
- except when

Avoid vague references:
- Do not use “the above”, “this”, “that condition”, or “the latter” when a named condition is clearer.
- Repeat the legal concept explicitly when needed.

================================================================================
LEGAL MODELING RULES
================================================================================

Model only the law.
Do not introduce concrete persons, organizations, objects, dates, periods, or IDs unless they are explicitly law-level categories in the legal text.

Distinguish these concept types:

1. Observable factual conditions:
   Facts that a future case could state directly.
   Examples in abstract form: a relationship exists, an event occurred, a document was submitted, a value is known, a period applies.

2. Derived legal results:
   Legal statuses, classifications, rights, obligations, permissions, prohibitions, sanctions, validity results, exclusions, exceptions, entitlements, or legal effects that follow from the law.

3. Helper conditions:
   Intermediate conditions used to express thresholds, exceptions, temporal requirements, combinations of criteria, or repeated subconditions.

Use separate sentences for each important derived legal result.
Do not collapse many different legal effects into one vague sentence.

================================================================================
ROLES AND RELATIONS
================================================================================

When a rule involves multiple roles, name the roles explicitly.
Do not use one generic subject for incompatible roles.

Good pattern:
- A legal result holds for actor A with respect to actor/object/event B if A has a required relation to B and B satisfies the required condition.

Bad pattern:
- A person has the legal result if the person is both role A and role B, when role A and role B are usually different participants.

Use stable role wording throughout the LE text.
If the law uses several terms for the same role, choose one clear term and use it consistently.

================================================================================
THRESHOLDS, VALUES, DATES, AND PERIODS
================================================================================

For measurable legal criteria, state the raw value and comparison clearly:
- the number of X is less than or equal to N;
- the amount of Y is greater than N;
- the period lasts at least N days;
- the event occurs before/after/on a relevant date.

Use one unit consistently for each quantity type when conversion is obvious from the law.
Do not invent conversions if the legal meaning depends on the original unit.

For “at least two of A, B, C”, write the condition explicitly:
- A and B, or A and C, or B and C.

For “at most one of A, B, C”, write:
- not at least two of A, B, and C hold.

================================================================================
EXCEPTIONS AND DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

For definitions:
- State the defined legal concept explicitly.
- State the factual or helper conditions that define it.

For exceptions:
- State the main rule.
- State the exception separately.
- Make clear whether the exception blocks, removes, limits, suspends, or modifies the legal result.

Do not assume an exception applies unless the legal text says so.

================================================================================
MINIMALITY AND FAITHFULNESS
================================================================================

Include only concepts supported by the law text.
Do not add policy assumptions, common-sense exceptions, or external legal knowledge.
If the law text is ambiguous, preserve the ambiguity rather than inventing a precise rule.
If a provision cannot be formalized cleanly, state the closest explicit legal condition without guessing.

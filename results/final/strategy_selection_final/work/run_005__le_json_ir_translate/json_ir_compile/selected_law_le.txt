A person is an alien if the person does not provide proof that they possess Belgian nationality.

A person is a third-country national if the person is not a Union citizen and the person is not a person enjoying the Community right of free movement as defined in Article 2, point 5, of the Schengen Borders Code.

An alien has illegal stay if the alien is present on the territory and the alien does not meet or no longer meets the conditions for entry to or stay on the territory.

An alien is an identified alien if the alien is in possession of a valid travel document, a valid passport, or a valid identity document.

An alien is an identified alien if the alien has been recognised as a national by the national authority of their country and that authority has declared itself willing to issue a laissez-passer.

An alien is an identified alien if the alien falls within the category of nationalities for which the minister may personally issue a laissez-passer.

A risk of absconding exists for an alien if the alien is the subject of a removal procedure, a procedure for the granting of international protection, or a procedure for determining or transferring to the Member State responsible for examining the application for international protection, and there are reasons to believe that the alien will abscond, having regard to the criteria listed in paragraph 2.

A risk of absconding must be current and real.

A risk of absconding is established following an individual examination and on the basis of one or more objective criteria listed in paragraph 2, taking into account all circumstances specific to each case.

The no-residence-application criterion is met for a person if the person did not submit a residence application following their illegal entry or during their illegal stay, or did not make their application for international protection within the time limit provided for by this Act.

The false-information criterion is met for a person if the person used false or misleading information or false or forged documents in the context of a procedure for international protection, residence, removal, or refusal of entry, or committed fraud or used other unlawful means in such a procedure.

The non-cooperation criterion is met for a person if the person does not cooperate or has not cooperated in the context of their relations with the authorities responsible for the implementation of or supervision of compliance with the regulations concerning access to the territory, residence, establishment, and removal of aliens.

The non-compliance criterion is met for a person if the person has made clear that they do not wish to comply with a transfer, refusal-of-entry, or removal measure, or has already failed to comply with such a measure.

The non-compliance criterion is met for a person if the person has made clear that they do not wish to comply with an entry ban that has neither been lifted nor suspended, or has already failed to comply with such a ban.

The non-compliance criterion is met for a person if the person has made clear that they do not wish to comply with a less coercive measure than a deprivation of liberty measure aimed at guaranteeing their transfer, refusal of entry, or removal, regardless of whether it is a liberty-restricting measure or another measure, or has already failed to comply with such a measure.

The non-compliance criterion is met for a person if the person has made clear that they do not wish to comply with a liberty-restricting measure aimed at guaranteeing public order or national security, or has already failed to comply with such a measure.

The non-compliance criterion is met for a person if the person has made clear that they do not wish to comply with a measure taken by another Member State equivalent to a transfer measure, a refusal-of-entry measure, a removal measure, an entry ban, a less coercive measure, or a liberty-restricting measure, or has already failed to comply with such a measure.

The entry-ban criterion is met for a person if the person is the subject of an entry ban in the Kingdom or in another Member State that has neither been lifted nor suspended.

The new-application criterion is met for a person if the person submitted a new residence application or a new application for international protection immediately after having been the subject of a decision refusing entry or residence, a decision terminating their stay, or a refusal-of-entry or removal measure.

The concealment-of-fingerprints criterion is met for a person if, while being questioned on that point, the person concealed the fact that they had already provided their fingerprints in another State bound by the European regulations concerning the determination of the State responsible for examining an application for international protection, after having submitted an application for international protection.

The multiple-applications criterion is met for a person if the person submitted multiple applications for international protection or multiple residence applications in the Kingdom or in one or more other Member States, which have given rise to a negative decision or which have not resulted in the issuance of a residence permit.

The concealment-of-prior-application criterion is met for a person if, while being questioned on that point, the person concealed the fact that they had previously submitted an application for international protection in another State bound by the European regulations concerning the determination of the State responsible for examining an application for international protection.

The declared-other-purpose criterion is met for a person if the person declared, or it appears from their file, that they came to the Kingdom for purposes other than those for which they submitted an application for international protection or a residence application.

The manifestly-unlawful-appeal criterion is met for a person if the person is the subject of a fine because they submitted a manifestly unlawful appeal to the Council for Alien Law Litigation.
